The Problem

It is not rare to see stray cats and dogs roaming the streets, whether in your own neighborhood or across different countries. These animals come from various backgrounds: some were abandoned by their owners, while others were born into life on the streets and forced to endure hunger, illness, and exposure.

In the Philippines, stray animal estimates have risen from about 12 million in 2019 to over 13 million as of 2024 (Philippine News Agency, 2023; Mindanews, 2025; Manila Times, 2025). These large numbers pose a critical public health threat, particularly in relation to rabies, which remains a lethal risk in areas with limited vaccination coverage.

Efforts to Address the Issue

In response to this crisis, several initiatives are being pursued in the Philippines. Senator Grace Poe has proposed the establishment of a specialized animal welfare bureau under the Department of Agriculture, while Senator Mark Villar authored Senate Bill 2257 to grant tax incentives for adopters of stray animals.

Organizations such as the Philippine Animal Welfare Society (PAWS) continue to advocate for wider rabies vaccination campaigns. However, financial and logistical challenges still prevent reaching the recommended 70% vaccination coverage. These efforts show that while progress is being made, much more work is needed.
